#Definitions

Polymerase Chain Reaction (PCR) is a molecular biology technique used to rapidly amplify a specific segment of DNA, producing millions to billions of copies from a very small starting sample. It is one of the most foundational tools in modern biology and medicine.

A PCR reaction works by cycling through three temperature-controlled steps repeatedly. First, denaturation at around 95°C separates double-stranded DNA into two single strands. Next, annealing at roughly 50–65°C allows short synthetic DNA sequences called primers to bind to the target region on each strand. Finally, extension at around 72°C allows a heat-stable enzyme called [[Definitions/Polymerase\|DNA polymerase]] — most commonly Taq polymerase — to synthesize a new complementary copy of each strand. Each cycle approximately doubles the amount of target DNA, so after 30–40 cycles the amplification is exponential. A full run typically takes one to three hours.

PCR is used across a wide range of applications: genetic testing, disease diagnosis (including COVID-19 tests), forensic analysis, research cloning, and more. It is essentially indispensable to modern molecular biology.

Promega is a major commercial supplier of PCR reagents and kits. Products include GoTaq and GoTaq Green mastermixes, Taq DNA Polymerase, dNTPs (the individual nucleotide building blocks), and specialized [[Definitions/Polymerase\|polymerases]] for high-fidelity or long-range PCR applications.
