#Definitions

A Resistance Temperature Detector (RTD) is a temperature sensor that works by measuring the change in electrical resistance of a material — typically platinum — as temperature changes. As temperature rises, resistance increases in a known, repeatable, and nearly linear way, allowing for precise temperature measurement. Platinum RTDs are preferred for industrial applications because platinum is stable, does not oxidize easily, and covers a wide temperature range (roughly -200°C to 850°C). RTDs offer higher accuracy and long-term stability compared to thermocouples, which is why they are increasingly used in industrial settings below 600°C. In my work, RTDs came up as the probe type being calibrated in the [[My Projects/Calibration Probe Fixture\|Calibration Probe Fixture]] project.

>
> **Common construction:** Platinum wire coil or thin film on a ceramic substrate
>
> **Typical resistance:** 100 ohms at 0°C (Pt100 standard)
